Choosing the right food is the single most important decision you'll make for your
cat's health. In this guide we break down what actually matters on the label, then
share our top picks after weeks of testing.

## What to look for in cat food

Cats are **obligate carnivores** — they need meat. When reading a label, prioritize:

1. A named animal protein as the first ingredient (e.g. "chicken", not "meat meal").
2. Adequate moisture, especially for cats prone to urinary issues.
3. Complete and balanced certification for your cat's life stage.
4. Minimal fillers and no unnecessary artificial colors.

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch the carbs">
Many cheap dry foods are loaded with carbohydrates that cats simply don't need.
Aim for high-protein, low-carb formulas where possible.
</Callout>

## Wet vs. dry: which is better?

The short answer: a combination often works best. Wet food boosts hydration, while
dry food is convenient and good for free-feeding. Many owners do a mix of both.

## How much should you feed?

Feeding amounts depend on weight, age, and activity. Always start with the label's
guidance and adjust based on your cat's body condition. When in doubt, ask your vet.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How often should I feed my cat?

Most adult cats do well with two measured meals per day. Kittens need more frequent
feeding — typically three to four small meals daily.

### Is grain-free food necessary?

Not for every cat. Grain-free can help cats with specific sensitivities, but grains
themselves aren't inherently bad. Focus on overall protein quality.

### Can I mix wet and dry food?

Yes. Mixing is a great way to combine the hydration benefits of wet food with the
convenience of dry food.
